Part 1 of 2The User Signing Surface

As protocol-layer exploits became harder, attackers shifted to the softest remaining surface: end users. Wallet drainers trick users into signing transactions or messages they don't understand — turning a single signature into a full wallet drain. This section covers the drainer economy, the six primary transaction categories, and the AngelFerno distribution campaign that has dominated Q1 2026.

Threat modelHow Drainers Reach Users

Drainers are a product category with real engineering investment. Operators don't write malicious code — they buy it, then focus on distribution.

01
Kit Purchase
Operator buys a drainer SDK — JavaScript components that generate malicious transactions.
02
Brand Impersonation
Clone of a known dApp, or a hijacked domain of a real one. Often with verified-looking contracts.
03
Distribution
Paid ads, phishing email, DMs, compromised community channels — or registrar social engineering.
04
Signature Capture
User lands, connects wallet, and is prompted to sign a drainer-crafted tx or message.
05
Drain
Funds are swept — immediately on transfer drains, later on approvals and permits.

The SDK economy changes how we defend

Because the same kits are reused across hundreds of sites, static fingerprints of their JavaScript and network behavior let us flag new malicious dApps before a single user signs. Drainer kits also advertise "Blockaid bypass" claims in their sales channels — a signal we actively monitor.

TaxonomyThe Six Drainer Transaction Categories

Severity: High — immediate drain Medium — delayed drain Context-dependent
01 · Native Transfer

Wrong-Address Transfer

User signs what looks like a normal ETH/SOL/BNB transfer, but the recipient is an attacker-controlled EOA. Calldata is empty, so there is nothing to parse — detection depends entirely on counterparty reputation.

Key signals: recipient age on-chain, distinct-sender count, originating dApp reputation, value relative to wallet balance.
High severity Native asset EOA recipient
02 · Token Transfer

Transfer Farming

ERC-20 analog of the above. The dApp prompts an outright transfer of the user's token balance — often the full holdings — to an attacker address.

Key signals: recipient reputation, percent of user's token balance transferred, unusual batching across tokens.
High severity ERC-20 Full-balance drain
03 · Allowance Abuse

Approval Farming

User grants a malicious spender an approval — often unlimited. Nothing leaves the wallet in the moment, which is exactly why it works. The drain happens at the attacker's convenience.

Key signals: spender is an EOA (no code to audit), spender reputation, unlimited allowance, no matching legit protocol.
High severity Approval exposure Delayed drain
04 · Off-Chain Signature

Permit / EIP-712 Farming

Stealthier variant of approval farming. Instead of an on-chain approval, the user signs an EIP-712 message (e.g., permit) off-chain. Many scanners ignore signed messages entirely.

Key signals: Blockaid simulates the on-chain transaction the signature would enable and evaluates it like any other approval.
High severity EIP-712 Off-chain
05 · Contract Disguise

Stealthy Malicious Contracts

A contract with verified, human-readable source is deployed to add a veneer of legitimacy. The trick hides in plain sight — e.g., a claim function that credits an arbitrary recipient parameter instead of msg.sender.

Key signals: simulated balance changes don't match user intent, arbitrary-sender patterns in calldata, novel contracts surfacing across known malicious dApps.
High severity Verified source Semantic trick
06 · UI Layer

Address Spoofing

Because wallets truncate long addresses, attackers generate vanity addresses whose visible portion (first/last characters) matches a familiar counterparty — or the burn address. A UI-layer attack that signature review alone cannot catch.

Key signals: prefix/suffix collision with prior counterparties, lookalike of zero-address, transfer-to-self patterns that aren't actually self.
Medium severity UX exploit Vanity address

Case studies · Wallet drainersThe AngelFerno DNS Hijack Campaign

AngelFerno (a.k.a. Inferno) is the dominant drainer family tracked internally. The operators have industrialized registrar and DNS social engineering — instead of building fake lookalike domains, they hijack the real ones. Users visit the legitimate URL, see a valid TLS certificate, and get served a drainer-laced frontend. Our threat-intel team has documented five confirmed AngelFerno hijacks in the last sixty days.

Case 01 · Feb 16, 2026

OpenEden + Curvance — GoDaddy social engineering

Vector: Registrar account takeover Loss: contained by Blockaid partners

Attackers socially engineered a GoDaddy employee to gain unauthorized access to both project accounts. Domains were transferred to Namecheap, NS records pointed at malicious IPs on web-hosting.com (162.254.39.77, 199.188.201.151), and fresh SSL certs were issued same-day to maintain valid HTTPS. Both frontends served AngelFerno drainer JavaScript.

Blockaid impact: dApp scanning flagged the malicious JS before drainer SDK could execute. Blockaid-protected wallets across all integrations remained safe through the incident window.
Case 02 · Mar 30, 2026

Steakhouse Financial — OVH → Hostinger

Vector: Registrar social engineering Loss: pre-empted by Blockaid alert

Between 05:00–10:00 UTC, every DNS record for steakhouse.financial was replaced. Nameservers moved from OVH (stable since April 2022) to Hostinger's dns-parking.com, the A record moved to shared hosting in Germany, MX records moved off Google Workspace, and a new Let's Encrypt cert was issued at 08:57 UTC. Attacker gained full control over web and email.

Blockaid impact: Proactive detection — we notified Steakhouse and partner wallets before user losses materialized.
Case 03 · Apr 14, 2026

CoW Swap — Registry-level takeover

Vector: Social engineering of .fi TLD registry + AWS registrar User losses: ~$1.2M

The most sophisticated AngelFerno incident to date. Attackers impersonated a senior CoW DAO contributor to Traficom (Finnish TLD registry) and Gandi (AWS Route 53's .fi registrar) using forged identity documents, altered company records, fabricated AWS support chat transcripts, and AI-generated financial documents. After the registrar-held email was changed at 12:16 UTC, the domain was transferred to NETIM, pointed at attacker Cloudflare nameservers, and a wildcard Let's Encrypt cert was issued. Users visiting cow.fi got a phishing replica served from LiteSpeed (distinct from CoW's Vercel stack) that harvested wallet connections and seed phrases via fake unlock modals.

SE relevance: AI-forged paperwork is now part of the playbook. Customers relying on registrar account hygiene alone are exposed — dApp scanning is the compensating control.
Case 04 · Apr 17, 2026

eth.limo — Escalation to shared infrastructure

Vector: EasyDNS registrar account compromise Loss: none — blocked by DNSSEC

Attackers socially engineered eth.limo's EasyDNS account and pointed NS records first through Cloudflare, then to Namecheap-hosted attacker infrastructure. Because eth.limo publishes DNSSEC, validating resolvers returned SERVFAIL instead of the attacker's records. No user impact confirmed. The strategic significance: eth.limo is an ENS gateway — a successful hijack would have simultaneously weaponized every ENS-hosted frontend behind it. AngelFerno is now escalating from per-project targets to shared crypto infrastructure.

Talk track: "DNSSEC is the single control that saved eth.limo. <1% of DeFi projects publish it. dApp scanning is the layer that catches it when they don't."

What's new in the AngelFerno playbook

Five confirmed hijacks against GoDaddy, Squarespace, Gandi (via Traficom), EasyDNS. AI-generated forgery is routine. Fallback phishing domains (e.g., swap.cow-s.fo on the Faroe Islands TLD) are pre-registered. Wildcard Let's Encrypt certs are issued within minutes. Blockaid's dApp scanning catches the drainer SDK regardless of how the user arrived at the page — the defense is resilient to any amount of registrar-side cleverness.

Part 2 of 2The Protocol Layer

Q1 2026 ended the narrative that "smart contracts are audited, so protocol hacks are solved." More than $577M was drained in two incidents alone, with structural gaps in off-chain operations, bridge verification, oracle configuration, and proxy governance. This section walks the four defining incidents and maps each to the Blockaid product that would have prevented it.

Case 05 · April 1, 2026Drift Protocol — $285M

Solana-based perpetuals DEX. Off-chain key compromise, not a smart-contract bug.

What happened

Vector: Squads multisig signer compromise Loss: ~$285M (second-largest Solana hack ever) Attribution: DPRK / Lazarus (same cluster as Radiant Oct 2024)

Drift's treasury was secured by a Squads multisig — the Solana equivalent of a Gnosis Safe. Attackers compromised enough signer devices (malware via fake job offers / poisoned dev tools) to hit the approval threshold. With valid signatures in hand, they submitted a single transaction draining three vaults. Complete in under one minute. Funds reached Binance within hours.

Chainalysis took ~9 hours to flag the transaction. Blockaid's pre-sign detection fires in ~0.3 seconds.

How Blockaid would have prevented it

Product: Cosigner

Cosigner sits between the multisig and the blockchain. Before any signature is committed, it decodes the actual transaction bytes — not what the wallet UI claims — and checks: is the destination new, is the value abnormal, has this pattern ever been seen before? A transaction draining the entire treasury to a never-before-seen address would have been refused pre-signature.

The internal pitch: "Drift = why you need a cosigner on privileged key operations. Radiant Capital (Oct 2024) was the same group, same pattern, same fix."
SE talk track: Anytime a prospect mentions a multisig, Fireblocks, Copper, BitGo, Squads, or any self-custodied treasury — open with Drift. "Your contracts are fine. The question is what happens when an attacker has the signatures."

Case 06 · April 18, 2026KelpDAO rsETH Bridge — $292M

LayerZero-based cross-chain bridge. Infrastructure compromise at the verifier layer.

What happened

Vector: Compromised RPC + DDoS failover → forged cross-chain packet Loss: ~$292M rsETH (largest DeFi hack of 2026) Bad debt on Aave: ~$177M

KelpDAO's LayerZero bridge was configured with a 1-of-1 DVN (Decentralized Verifier Network) — a single verifier with no backup signer. Attackers compromised two of the verifier's upstream RPC nodes, then DDoSed the primary to force failover onto the poisoned backup. With the verifier now reading attacker-controlled data, they sent a forged lzReceive packet claiming 116,500 rsETH had been locked on Ethereum. No lock had happened. The bridge released rsETH on 20+ destination chains — minting value from thin air. Attackers then posted the counterfeit rsETH as collateral on Aave, Compound, and Euler to borrow an additional $236M.

How Blockaid would have prevented it

Product: Monitoring & Risk Exposure

Bridge-layer monitoring reconciles the supply of a wrapped asset on destination chains against locked collateral on the origin chain. A 116,500-rsETH divergence with no matching deposit would have fired within seconds. For the lending protocols holding rsETH as collateral (Aave, Compound, Euler), a pause trigger on that anomaly would have blocked the downstream $236M bad-debt cascade.

The internal pitch: "KelpDAO = why every protocol that depends on a bridged asset needs independent bridge monitoring, and why 1-of-1 DVNs are negligence."
SE talk track: Use KelpDAO with any protocol operating or depending on LayerZero, Wormhole, CCTP, or any cross-chain messaging. Also effective with lending markets — Aave's $8.45B TVL outflow in 48 hours is the contagion argument.

Case 07 · February 15, 2026Moonwell on Base — $918K

Compound V2 fork on Base. Oracle feed misconfiguration.

What happened

Vector: Wrong Chainlink feed (cbETH/ETH ratio instead of cbETH/USD) Loss: 468.63 ETH (~$918K) Drain time: <1 minute

Chainlink publishes two feeds for cbETH: a USD price feed (~$2,216) and an ETH ratio feed (~1.12). Moonwell's ChainlinkOracle was written for USD feeds only — no handling for ETH-denominated ratios. An admin pointed the cbETH feed at the ratio (1.12) instead of the USD price (2,216). The oracle reported cbETH as worth $1.12 — 1,974x undervalued. Attacker flash-loaned $565 of WETH, deposited as collateral, borrowed $925,614 of cbETH (valued by the oracle at $468), swapped on Uniswap at real prices, repaid the flash loan, kept $918K.

How Blockaid would have prevented it

Product: Monitoring & Risk Exposure

Price sanity monitoring on lending markets fires on oracle values outside expected bands. "The cbETH oracle is returning $1.12" would be flagged within seconds — long before an attacker could complete 67 days of pre-positioning. Additionally, our pre-tx validation API correctly classified the real exploit transaction as exploit_contract_execution / severity severe.

The internal pitch: "Moonwell is the cleanest case for lending protocols. Not a novel attack. Not a smart-contract bug. An admin picked the wrong feed from a dropdown — the exact thing a sanity check catches."
SE talk track: Use Moonwell with any lending market — Aave, Compound, Morpho, Silo, Euler, Radiant, Spark, Lista, Moonwell itself. Also any protocol that has admin-configurable price feeds. Pair with Rhea Finance ($7.6M, fake token oracle manipulation, Apr 16) and Blend on Stellar (Reflector oracle, Apr 2026) for pattern reinforcement.

Case 08 · January 8 & 27, 2026Dinari usdPlus — Proxy Takeover

Tokenized-stocks platform on Base. Uninitialized proxy front-run.

What happened

Vector: Uninitialized proxy + malicious spoofed-upgrade impl Loss: Backdoor on usdPlus + 2 other proxies Follow-up: 3 silent state-modification txs 3 weeks later

Dinari deployed proxy contracts without immediately calling initialize(). An attacker bot spotted the unclaimed proxy in the mempool, front-ran Dinari with their own initialize() call to become owner, and immediately upgraded the proxy to a malicious implementation. The sophisticated twist: the attacker's implementation was designed to spoof subsequent upgrade events — if Dinari tried to "fix" the proxy, the event logs would show a clean upgrade but storage would remain unchanged, leaving the backdoor live while appearing remediated.

How Blockaid would have prevented it

Product: Cosigner + Monitoring

Cosigner inspects privileged transactions including initialize() and upgradeTo(). Any initialize() call from an address with no prior relationship to the protocol, or any upgrade pointing at an unverified freshly-deployed implementation, would be refused pre-signature. Separately, our proxy-upgrade monitoring tracks EIP-1967 implementation slots and fires on storage changes that don't match emitted events — the spoofed-upgrade trick.

The internal pitch: "Dinari is the textbook 2026 proxy hack. Same shape as Parity 2017, but with a spoofed-upgrade layer that would fool any team relying on Etherscan events to verify remediation."
SE talk track: Use Dinari with any protocol using upgradeable contracts — which is most of them. Pair with Radiant Capital (Oct 2024, same Lazarus cluster as Drift) for the signer-compromise version of the same story.

ArchitectureThe Five Detection Layers

No single layer covers the full surface. Blockaid's defense is a set of reinforcing checks across the user, the dApp, the transaction, the protocol, and the asset. Each layer closes blind spots the others leave open.

Transaction Scanning

Simulates the actual effects of a signature — asset diffs, approvals, exposures, exit paths — rather than parsing calldata.

  • Catches raw transfers, approvals, permits, arbitrary-recipient contracts.
  • Covers on-chain transactions and off-chain EIP-712 messages.
  • Reputation context on every address and token involved.

dApp Scanning

Evaluates the site — JavaScript fingerprints, network behavior, brand-impersonation signals — before any transaction is signed.

  • Flags drainer-SDK reuse across thousands of sites.
  • Catches DNS hijacks of legitimate domains (see AngelFerno).
  • Surfaces malicious dApps even when the tx they emit looks clean.

Cosigner

Independent inspection of privileged transactions before they're signed by a multisig or custody stack.

  • Decodes actual transaction bytes, not what the wallet UI claims.
  • Blocks upgrade-to-malicious-impl, drain-to-new-address, and unauthorized privilege grants.
  • Integrations: Fireblocks, Safe, Squads, BitGo, Copper, custom.

Monitoring & Risk Exposure

Continuous surveillance of protocol state — oracles, bridges, liquidity, admin actions.

  • Oracle price sanity bands (see Moonwell).
  • Bridge supply/lock reconciliation (see KelpDAO).
  • Admin-action alerts and automated pause triggers.

Token Scanning

Rug-pull, honeypot, and malicious-token detection on the asset itself.

  • Admin powers, unlocked LP, concentrated supply, unverified code.
  • Honeypot simulation (can the token actually be sold?).
  • Consumed by major wallets and exchange listing flows.

Threat Intel Feed

The input that keeps all five layers current.

  • Drainer family tracking (AngelFerno, RublevkaTeam, Purple, Quark, Noir, Impala).
  • DNS hijack campaigns, frontend compromises, IOC publication.
  • Proactive notifications to affected partners (see Steakhouse, OpenEden).

The feedback loop

When a known-malicious dApp emits a transaction that looks benign, we investigate a potential transaction-rule bypass. When a reputable dApp emits a transaction that looks malicious, we investigate a potential compromise. When a legitimate protocol's privileged transaction triggers a cosigner refusal, we investigate a potential key compromise. The combination is what keeps pace with weekly drainer iteration and quarterly protocol-hack waves.

AppendixWhat This Means for a Wallet Team

Simulation is not optional

A calldata-only view cannot distinguish an empty transfer to a malicious EOA from a benign send, cannot surface off-chain permit risk, and cannot catch stealthy contract patterns. Simulation is the baseline for meaningful user warnings.

Reputation scales where rules don't

Drainer kits change weekly; reputation signals accumulated across millions of wallet interactions do not. The breadth of Blockaid's integrations is a direct input into detection quality.

Both layers, or neither

Shipping transaction scanning without dApp scanning (or vice versa) leaves coverage gaps that drainers actively exploit. The two should be deployed as a package.

Feedback matters

A reporting endpoint for false positives and false negatives is part of the product. User- and integrator-reported cases feed directly into our threat intel pipeline.
